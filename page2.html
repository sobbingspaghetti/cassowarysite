Who is this guy?

This is a Southern Cassowary, a large
flightless bird native to Australia
and some parts of Papua New Guinea.
They are known as ‘the world’s most
dangerous bird,’ however theyare
fairly docile unless provoked.

They are actually the genetically
closest living avian relative of
dinosaurs. Click here to learn
more about their connection.

These are the three subspecies of cassowary
Southern Cassowary
Dwarf Cassowary
Northern Cassowary

What makes them so special?

Cassowaries have long, sharp claws
that earn them the title of ‘world’s
most dangerous bird,’ the force
behind one of their kicks is enough to
gut a mammal-- including humans.
They are also capable of running up to 30mph.
Please don’t run from these guys.

They are also considered a ‘keystone
species’ in Northern Queensland due
to their fruit diet-- they spread
these seeds throughout the rainforest
and are essential for the survival of
up to 238 rainforest plant species.

Why should I care?

Cassowaries are a threatened species,
with an estimated wild population of
4,600 Southern Cassowaries left. Out
of the three subspecies of cassowary,
they are the most threatened.

Thankfully, there are efforts in place
to protect the cassowary, however
this doesn’t mean they will be off
the endangered list anytime soon.
Here is a website for a cassowary
conservation group, check it out if
you’re interested!
